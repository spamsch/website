AGI — the holy grail of AI. Billions in funding chase it. Every keynote promises it's "2–3 years away." And nobody can agree on what it actually means.

Then Yann LeCun called general intelligence "complete BS." Demis Hassabis fired back: "just plain incorrect." Elon Musk picked a side. Two Turing Award winners, publicly clashing over whether the goal the entire industry is racing toward even exists.

But the most useful thing LeCun said isn't the provocation. It's this: "Human intelligence is not general at all. It's extremely specialized."

++ The definitional mess

There is no agreed definition of AGI. OpenAI says "systems that outperform humans at most economically useful tasks." DeepMind says "all cognitive capabilities humans can exhibit." Anthropic's CEO calls AGI "a marketing term." Sam Altman said in late 2025 that "we built AGIs" — while 76% of leading AI researchers in an AAAI survey said scaling current approaches is "unlikely" to yield AGI.

When the people building these systems can't agree on what the goal is, the term is a Rorschach test.

++ What LeCun actually proposes

His five positions boil down to: stop chasing a vague "general" benchmark. Human intelligence is specialized. Generality isn't required for usefulness. Focus instead on Superhuman Adaptable Intelligence — systems that learn world models, predict how the physical world behaves, and adapt to new tasks without retraining on millions of examples.

Hassabis counters that LeCun confuses "general" with "universal" — brains are approximate Turing machines, general enough to learn anything computable. Fair theoretical point. But it doesn't change the practical one.

++ Why this matters if you ship software

The AGI debate is interesting philosophy. But if you're building AI products today, the lesson is clear: stop designing for generality and start designing for adaptability.

- The hard problem isn't model intelligence — it's system architecture. Can your pipeline onboard a new domain in days, not months? That's retrieval design, data ingestion, and knowledge management — not model scale.
- Reliability beats capability. A system that's right 95% of the time on a narrow task ships. A "general" system that's right 80% across everything doesn't. Production rewards consistency, not range.
- Adaptability is an architecture property, not a model property. It lives in how fast you can swap a retrieval index, fine-tune on new data, or reroute tasks — not in one endpoint that does it all.

++ Where I stand

The AGI debate generates headlines. Specialized, adaptable systems generate ROI. The systems shipping value today look nothing like a single general intelligence — they look like an ecosystem of purpose-built models, each doing one thing well.

The question isn't "when will we get AGI?" It's "what's the right mix of specialized capabilities for my workloads?" That one has answers you can act on today.
